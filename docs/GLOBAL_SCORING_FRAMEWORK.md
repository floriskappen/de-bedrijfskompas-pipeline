# de bedrijfskompas — Scoring Framework

*A constitution for how this project judges companies.*

---

## Why this exists

Most job platforms are vacancy-first. They show you companies that happen to
have an opening, dressed in recruitment-marketing language, and ask you to react
to them. They solve the wrong problem. You end up learning what a company *says
to candidates*, not what it actually does or stands for.

This project turns the flow around. It starts from the company — what it really
does, stripped of marketing — and lets a person find places worth working for,
rather than waiting for places to advertise at them. Vacancies are not the
enemy; vacancy-*first* is. Open roles are one lens among several, applied late,
never the gatekeeper.

To make companies comparable without flattening them, the project judges each
one on five structural axes. These axes describe *what kind of thing a company
is* — not which topics it works on, and not whether it is currently hiring. They
are deliberately macro: they ask what is genuinely good for the world, beyond
the question of whether the company makes money.

---

## Founding principles

These hold across everything that follows.

**Public data only.** The prototype judges a company solely on what it makes
publicly available — primarily its own website. This keeps the scope honest and
the limitations visible. We never pretend to know more than the public record
shows.

**Evidence levels, not just scores.** Every axis score carries a confidence
level: *well-evidenced*, *partial*, or *no signal*. A company is not "bad" on an
axis because its website is silent — it is *unknown*. Silence is data, not a
failing grade. This is the difference between an honest tool and a black box.

**Silence is read differently on different axes.** On some axes, vagueness is
itself a negative signal — a company being evasive about what it actually does.
On others, silence is normal and must not be punished. Each axis below states
how to read its silence.

**Profile over single score.** A company is shown as a profile across the five
axes, with evidence levels visible. There is no fixed global composite score.
Collapsing five qualitative judgments into one number is exactly the kind of
flattening this project exists to resist — and it reproduces the vacancy-platform
sin of hiding structure behind a single ranking. Where a sortable composite *is*
wanted, the only honest one is personalised: the user's own priorities applied
to the objective axis scores. There is no neutral aggregation. There is only
*your* aggregation, out in the open.

**The axes are objective; the weighting is where values live.** The five axes
try to describe a company as fairly and consistently as possible. What a person
*does with* those scores — which axes they care about — is personal, and belongs
to them, transparently, not buried in a hidden formula.

---

## The five axes

Each axis has: a plain-language meaning (no jargon), what it captures, how to
read it from a company's public text, and how to treat silence.

---

### 1. Substance

**In plain words:** *Does this company actually make the world a bit better, or
at least not worse? If it vanished tomorrow, would anyone genuinely be worse off
— or is it just moving money around, selling things nobody needs, or keeping
people hooked?*

**What it captures.** Whether the company's core activity meets a real need.
This is the question most certifications avoid: a company can sell something
pointless or harmful in a very ethical way. Substance asks about the thing
itself, not the manner of doing it.

**How to read it.** Strip away the mission language and the marketing and ask one
hard question: does the company's actual work genuinely make the world better —
improve lives, solve a real problem, meet a real need, heal or restore — or does
it merely earn money and make existing commercial activity faster and more
convenient? Be skeptical. Most companies, assessed honestly, add no genuine
positive value; the world would be no worse off without them. So most belong at or
below the neutral line, and a positive reading has to be earned — this is not an
axis to grade on a curve. A company reads as genuinely positive only when
bettering the world is its *core purpose*, not a side effect: restoring nature,
renewable energy, care for the sick or vulnerable, food/repair/housing people
truly need, art made with disabled people, education that is genuinely necessary.
Merely-commercial work sits *below* neutral, not at it — generic agencies and
consultancies, B2B productivity and efficiency tooling, software built because it
sells: legitimate, but contributing nothing, so the world would not miss them. The
bottom is actively harmful: attention-harvesting (adtech, addictive apps),
manufactured need, pure financial intermediation, predatory models (gambling,
payday lending, dark patterns). Legitimacy, professionalism, efficiency, and a
sector merely *adjacent* to a real need (a "health" or "education" word in the
copy) earn nothing on their own. Judge the substance of what is actually done —
and for content businesses like games or media, what that content does and for
whom.

**Reading silence.** Vagueness counts *against*. If after a few minutes on the
site you still cannot say what the company actually does, that is itself a
signal — either thin substance or deliberate obscurity. Both matter.

**A note on objectivity.** This is the least objective of the five axes, by
design. Deciding whether something "meets a real need" — and treating *making
commerce faster, cheaper, or more convenient* as no contribution in itself —
encodes a worldview, not a neutral measurement. That is a deliberate value
position, applied consistently, but sitting inside what the rest of this document
calls the objective layer. Named here honestly so it is not mistaken for a
fact: the other four axes describe a company; this one also judges it.

**Readability:** high.

---

### 2. Ecology

**In plain words:** *What does this company do to the living world — the climate,
the land, the materials it burns through? Does its actual business help repair
things, sit roughly neutral, or quietly wreck stuff while talking nicely about
it?*

**What it captures.** The company's relationship to natural systems, on a
spectrum from regenerative through neutral to extractive. The emphasis is on what
the business *is*, not on the sustainability page it wrote about itself.

**How to read it.** Sector first, and sector almost always *suffices*: the single
biggest signal is what business the company fundamentally is, so a baseline can
always be inferred from its type even when it says nothing about the environment.
This axis is therefore never "no signal" — there is always a sector to read.
Specific claims and the evidence behind them only adjust the baseline. Positive:
circular / repair / reuse, renewable energy, regenerative practice. Roughly
neutral and only mildly positive: materially-light services (software, knowledge
work) — low footprint, but not actively regenerative. Negative: fossil-adjacent,
fast fashion, single-use, heavy logistics as the core activity — *plus* the
greenwashing tell: a high ratio of green words to green facts. Treat a sector-only
read as partial evidence; reserve well-evidenced for concrete, ideally
third-party-verified facts.

**Reading silence.** Silence about the environment is not "no signal" — the sector
already gives one, so score the baseline. "We care deeply about the planet" with
zero specifics is a mild negative against that baseline, not a positive — it is
the gap between marketing language and observable reality, worth surfacing in its
own right.

**Readability:** medium; noisy.

---

### 3. Power

**In plain words:** *Who actually owns this place and who gets a say? Is it a few
owners and investors pulling value out, or is it shared more fairly with the
people doing the work? (Often they won't tell you — and that silence is worth
noticing too.)*

**What it captures.** How value and decision-making are distributed inside the
company — ownership, governance, voice. Philosophically this is the most
important axis: who controls, and who benefits. Empirically it is the least
visible.

**How to read it.** Strong positive signals when present: cooperative
(*coöperatie*), employee ownership, steward-ownership or
*stichting-administratiekantoor* structures, stated pay transparency,
worker voice beyond the legal minimum, language of "we" rather than "talent."
Extractive signals: heavy investor/exit framing, venture-hypergrowth language,
people described as a resource.

**Reading silence.** Do **not** penalise silence here. Most companies say nothing
about their internal structure, and absence of evidence is not evidence of
extraction. Default to *unknown*; score only on real evidence. This axis earns
its place precisely because, when the signal *is* there, it reveals what no other
platform shows — and when it is absent, "they're not saying" is itself a quietly
honest result.

**Readability:** low.

---

### 4. Embeddedness

**In plain words:** *Is this company actually part of a place and a community —
rooted somewhere, connected to real people — or a placeless machine that could be
anywhere and would leave the moment it's cheaper elsewhere?*

**What it captures.** External rootedness. Distinct from Power: Power is how
things are distributed *inside*; Embeddedness is how connected the company is to
the world *outside* — place, community, supply chain, long-term relationships. A
worker co-op can still be placeless; a rooted family business can still be run
autocratically. The two axes do not collapse into each other.

**How to read it.** Ask whether real people in a community would recognise this
company and feel it adds something to where they live — or whether it is a faceless
operation that could be anywhere and serves whoever, wherever. Be critical, as with
Substance: most companies are *not* embedded — especially digital, IT, B2B, and
service firms that sell to anyone anywhere — so most belong below the line, and a
positive reading is earned. Merely having an office in a city, or selling to one
country, is not embeddedness. Positive: a local business woven into its
neighbourhood, work done with and for a specific community, local sourcing, durable
local relationships. Embeddedness is not the same as being small or single-city —
an organisation can be *globally* embedded if it is genuinely rooted in the many
communities where it operates (an NGO embedded in the places it is active; cultural
or people-helping work inside communities). Negative: "global platform," "scale
across every market," footloose "we serve anyone anywhere," or a tie to place that
amounts only to an HQ address.

**Reading silence.** Low, not neutral. A placeless or "anyone anywhere" company is
simply not rooted — score it low, not in the middle. This is descriptive, not a
moral failing, and only genuinely useful global things should make you hesitate.
Reserve "no signal" for when the source says nothing at all about place or
community.

**Readability:** medium.

---

### 5. Posture

**In plain words:** *What's the company's whole vibe and ambition? Desperate to
grow forever, scale fast, dominate, disrupt — running flat-out? Or content to be
the right size, do good work, and last? How a company talks about itself usually
gives this away completely.*

**What it captures.** The company's orientation toward growth and scale — whether
it is driven by relentless expansion or oriented toward sufficiency, quality, and
endurance. This is the axis that holds the project's philosophical centre: the
difference between a frantic, optimise-everything mode of operating and a calmer,
relational, durable one.

**How to read it.** A website is rhetoric, and posture is announced. Growth-maxed
signals: "hypergrowth," "disrupt," "10x," "dominate," "blitzscale," "category
leader," war and conquest metaphors, relentless expansion. Sufficiency signals:
"right size," craft, care, "built to last," quality and relationship over scale,
a calm and unhurried tone.

**Reading silence.** Rare — most companies reveal their posture readily. Where a
site is genuinely neutral in tone, score as middling rather than guessing.

**Distinctions worth keeping.** A genuinely useful medical-device company
(high Substance) can still be run as a frantic land-grab (aggressive Posture). A
growth-maxed solar company can score well on Ecology and aggressively on Posture.
Posture is about *how the company moves through the world*, independent of what it
sells or its footprint — though it often correlates with both.

**Readability:** high.

---

## How readable each axis is

This ordering is why evidence levels matter, and why silence is treated
asymmetrically:

| Axis          | Readability | How silence is read              |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Substance     | High        | Vagueness counts against; most score below 50 |
| Posture       | High        | Score middling if truly neutral  |
| Ecology       | Medium      | Vague green talk = mild negative  |
| Embeddedness  | Medium      | Placeless = low (not neutral); no blame |
| Power         | Low         | Never penalise; default unknown   |

---

## How the axes are used

**Profile-first.** A company is presented as its five-axis profile with evidence
levels — not as a ranking number.

**Per-axis filtering is the primary interaction.** A person sets minimums on the
axes they care about ("strong Substance and Ecology, the rest I don't mind") and
the list responds. This keeps structure visible instead of hidden behind a single
sort.

**Personalised composite, if any.** Should a single sortable number be wanted, it
is built from the person's own priorities applied to the objective axis scores —
never a house ranking presented as neutral truth.

---

## What does *not* belong on these axes

**Topics are tags, not axes.** Climate, animal welfare, surveillance, education,
and the like are *what a company works on*. The five axes describe *what kind of
thing a company is*. Topics live below the axes, as tags, and feed the personal
matching layer — not the global structure.

**Hiring is not an axis.** Whether a company has open roles, and which roles, is
a late-stage matching lens — never a gate on whether the company appears at all.
The flow is company-first by design.
